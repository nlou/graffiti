Not All Graffiti is Treated Equally

Cleaning Graffiti in Midtown Takes Twice As Long As the Lower East Side.

By Nicole Lou


(Map of New York City, divided into police precincts. Zoom in for neighborhood names.)

Here’s something else you might blame on tourists. It turns out that walking in Times Square is not only a drag, but the throngs of tourists and wax figure enthusiasts are making it hard to clean graffiti. In a year’s worth of data between February 2014 and February 2015, a reported case of graffiti took an average of 136 days to clean in Midtown South, or Police Precinct 14. Compare that to a zippy 55 days in the Lower East Side, and 83 days in Chinatown.


And yes, the 55 days it takes to clean a vandal's art in the Lower East Side is quite an accomplishment when you take into account the fact that there are only about 29 people who are charged with cleaning graffiti across the city, as part of Graffiti-Free NYC (a project of the Department of Sanitation, Economic Development Corporation, and the Mayor's Community Affairs Unit).

So that's 29 people watching out for the monuments and architecture used by 8.4 million inhabitants of the city. They use the same process across the board for the five boroughs. If a spray paint drawing is on a painted surface on private property, Graffiti-Free NYC comes in to paint over it. If it's on an unpainted surface, however, it will need to be treated with potassium hydroxide, then blasted by a strong stream of hot water.


What happened?

How can there be such discrete differences between adjacent precincts? Here is a closeup of three particular cases.


Case #1: Midtown South vs. The rest of Midtown

Midtown South is the embodiment of entertainment and shopping. Macy's, The Empire State Building, Times Square, Grand Central Terminal, and Koreatown make great tourist hubss...but graffiti takes more than 4 months to clean up in these parts. This precinct is also home to assorted skysrapers, such as the Met Life Building, so it is no surprise that there are people everywhere all the time. Graffiti in high, hard to reach places + non-stop foot traffic = graffiti sticks around longer.


Case #2: Downtown Brooklyn (Precinct 84) vs. Carroll Gardens/Red Hook/Cobble Hill (Precinct 76)

Downtown Brooklyn dominates its neighborhors to the south in terms of number of businesses and residents, in addition to being a transportation hub. Sheer foot traffic in this area is a possible deterrent to cleanup, hence the longer time it takes to clean up.


Case #3: The Curious Case of Staten Island

Graffiti in the northern and eastern parts of Staten Island take 102 days, on average, to clean. These also happen to be residential areas filed with apartment complexes and public housing. Compare that to the western end of the island, an area that has residential as well as several strip malls (including the Staten Island Mall). Here it takes just 70 days to clean up graffiti. Maybe having more businesses means more people keen to get graffiti off their property.
